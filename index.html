Abstract

Decentralising the creation, publication, and annotation of hypertext documents provides authors with a†technological guarantee for freedom of speech and independence of any publication authority. While the Web was designed as a¬†decentralised environment, individual authors still lack convenient tools to write and publish documents themselves, and to engage in social interactions with documents of others. We present Dokieli, a¬†decentralised, browser-based editing and annotation platform with built-in social interactions, on which people retain the ownership and storage of their data. The resulting "living" documents are interoperable and independent of Dokieli since they follow standards and best practices, such as HTML+RDFa for a¬†fine-grained semantic structure, Linked Data Platform for personal data storage, and Linked Data Notifications for updates. This article describes Dokieli‚Äôs architecture and implementation, proving that the current technology allows for advanced document authoring and interaction without a¬†single point of control. Dokieli thus acts as a¬†fully decentralised social machine for Web documents, owned solely by its users, thereby offering a¬†feasible and powerful alternative to centralized content management, blogging and other social platforms. Such an environment provides the right technological conditions for independent publication of scientific articles, news, and other works that benefit from diverse voices and open interactions.

Introduction

Necessity of decentralization (e.g., Facebook and censorship, scientific publication and slow progress)

The Internet and the Web were originally concieved as decentralized platforms, where every organization and individual can participate. However, both the Internet and the Web became increasingly centralized with less than 1% of the Internet and Web servers serving more than 99% of the content. See e.g. https://news.netcraft.com/archives/category/web-server-survey/ The main reason for the increasing centralization is rooted in technology. It is currently much easier and efficient to author, manage, publish and search large amounts of similarly structured content using a centralized platform. Wikipedia, Youtube and Facebook, for example, are centralized authoring, publishing and search platforms for encyclopedic articles, videos or social network content respectively. If we want to strengthen the decentralized nature of the Internet and Web again, we need to develop technology, which simplifies the decentralized authoring, management as well as exploration and search of Web content.
