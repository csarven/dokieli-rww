Abstract

Decentralising the creation, publication, and annotation of hypertext documents provides authors with a¬†technological guarantee for independence of any publication authority. While the Web was designed as a¬†decentralised environment, individual authors still lack convenient tools to write and publish documents themselves, and to engage in social interactions with documents of others. We present Dokieli, a¬†decentralised, browser-based editing and annotation platform with built-in support for social interactions, on which people retain the ownership and storage of their data. The resulting ‚Äúliving‚Äù documents are interoperable and independent of Dokieli since they follow standards and best practices, such as HTML+RDFa for a¬†fine-grained semantic structure, Linked Data Platform for personal data storage, and Linked Data Notifications for updates. This article describes Dokieli‚Äôs architecture and implementation, proving that the current technology allows for advanced document authoring and interaction without a¬†single point of control. Dokieli thus acts as a¬†fully decentralised, user-owned social machine for Web documents, owned solely by its users, thereby offering a¬†powerful alternative to centralised content management, blogging and other social platforms. Such an environment provides the right technological conditions for independent publication of scientific articles, news, and other works that benefit from diverse voices and open interactions.

Introduction

In this section, we establish why decentralisation is important, why it is currently missing, and what we're going to do about it.

In introduction or elsewhere, can we have another angle at (de)centralised approaches? For example, the differences between vendor, data, and application lock-ins.

While the Web was originally conceived as a¬†decentralised platform where every organisation and individual can participate, it became increasingly centralised with less than 1% of the servers serving more than 99% of the content. The main reason for this is rooted in technology: it is currently much easier and efficient to author, manage, publish, and search large amounts of similarly structured content using a centralised platform. Blogger, YouTube and Facebook, for example, are centralised authoring, publishing, and search platforms for blog posts, videos or social network content respectively.

However, independence of centralised platforms is a¬†necessity for ownership of published ideas, and to establish a¬†relation of trust. For example, Facebook has been accused of bias, false information, and censorship‚Äîbut rather than blaming this on any particular platform, we identify this as an unavoidable cause of centralisation. After all, there is a¬†continued tension between unrestricted publication rights on the one hand, and a¬†guarantee of balanced, verified information on the other. In a¬†fully decentralized setting, each source is filterless and responsible for its own quality and reputation, while others are free to selectively (dis-)trust certain sources using any mechanism they desire.

Decentralized authoring, publication, and annotation furthermore have the potential to impact areas in which centralisation currently determines the pace of evolution. Scientific publishing, for instance, is often bound to a¬†centralised review process. Instead, rigorous scientific discourse could be realised with an open, decentralised environment for the annotation of manuscripts, which can engage more people sooner. Trust then no longer stems from a¬†finite process with limited transparency, but is rather continuously assessed by repeated independent validation. Publication thereby becomes the starting point rather than the end point.

If we want to strengthen the decentralised nature of the Web again, we need to develop technologies to simplify the decentralised authoring, management, as well as exploration and search of Web content.

Documents use HTML5 Polyglot Markup to ensure that when served as (X)HTML respectively, they can be processed as either HTML or XML - useful in XML ecosystems and toolchains. RDFa is predominently used to represent the human-visible content to optimise its contextual use - avoids data duplication and hidden islands as is the case with alternative RDF serializations in HTML. Different stylesheets are available in CSS3 and can be triggered either through the Web browser's controls or dokieli's menu. Its JavaScript provides the means to access interactive features of the document, as well as dynamic read-write operations on remote data.

Through the application of progressive enhancement, dokieli articles, annotations, and notifications (by default in HTML+RDFa) can be accessible virtually from any Web server, irrespective of them being: accompanied with CSS or JavaScript; enabled or supported by consuming user-agents in a way that is both humand and machine-readable.

dokieli's approach to marking human-visible content in RDFa makes it possible to further decouple itself (i.e., the application that produced the data), from its potential reuse by other applications. Hence, users can use other applications on the data that was generated by dokieli. Thereby, dokieli can effectively remove itself as a dependency when it comes to data consumption and reuse.

While dokieli can potentially be read from any server or local filesystem, its write operations to external HTTP resources require JavaScript to be enabled in the user-agent. This moves the burden from servers (i.e., offering HTML forms and processing of the form submission) to the client.

Currently, dokieli uses the HTTP POST, PUT, and DELETE methods for its write-operations given that their affordances are clear i.e., responses to the request indicate if, where, and how the resources may be accessible (This is in contrast to SPARQL Update operations, which do not necessarily mean that the operation is reflected on the resolveable URLs... is this too much detail? can be it condensed?). HTTP POST is used to fulfill actions like sending an annotation to a public service, or a notification to remote inboxes. In these cases, the receivers decides on their final retrievable URLs. HTTP PUT is used for document-centric actions like: new, save and save as, reply, as well as for user's to save their own annotations at a storage space in which they are authorized against. Users can make their contributions and have them preserved in different ways: authors which control their own articles, parties that have authority over an article's notification inbox, as well as external annotators e.g., reviewers, commenters. dokieli enables different users to voice themselves without interfering or imposing centralised mechanisms e.g., editorial systems. A notification is sent to the article's inbox to inform the consumers of that inbox about that annotation. A typical consumption is where the annotation is shown as marginalia in the article. If for instance, the owners of the inbox (which may or may not be the authors of the article), find the annotation inappropriate, they can remove the notification itself and thereby removing its exposure through the inbox. Since the annotation is controlled independently by its author at their own Web space they preserve the right to retain it. dokieli uses Linked Data Notifications to help with the discovery of voices while each contributor retaining their own respective rights over the entities they create on the Web.

Deployment

dokieli employs two deployment approaches: single-page application and Web browser extension.

dokieli's presentational and behavioural code layers can be included in Web pages in order to trigger them as active single-page applications. It is a thin-client that allows different kinds of articles e.g., academic, blog posts, news, to be authored and annotated from within Web browsers, without necessarily having them deployed from a server, i.e., it can be used offline or on localhost. dokieli internally handles its content and structural representation through its own UI, where the underlying data is dynamically exchanged between servers. Articles, profiles and their contact information, notifications, annotations with different motivations, for instance, can be read and written ubiquitously to any Web space with available access controls.

The Web browser extension is a thin wrapper around dokieli's core code in order to embed itself in any HTML-based Web page on the Web. It inherits all of the features of a single-page application. While HTML based documents on the Web vary in their quality, dokieli's write operations generate well-formed HTML+RDFa and other RDF serializations where applicable. One of the primary utilities for the extension is to be able to annotate e.g., comment, bookmark, any part of a Web page, as well as social shares e.g., via notifications, consistently across any Web page, without having service dependency, or limitations of the Web page's UI.

There are of course also social implications of using a dokieli-style architecture: How do we enable and encourage positive behaviour? Some concerns in that regard include for example:

  • How can harassment and abuse be prevented or handled? How would moderation or civil discourse work?
  • How can a resource state that it gives consent to be annotated at fine granularity e.g., liking, bookmarking are permitted, resharing, replying are not? How can the conditions to annotate be indicated e.g., the reply should be notified at inbox A, and have a copy sent also to inbox B? How can another resource's annotation policy/rules be respected e.g., some resource opts-out of annotations, so dokieli should not be in-reply-to?
  • How can anonymity, pseudonymity, individual, and global identity be taken into account? How can domain expertise or reputation be factored in?
However, it is also a question to what extent can or should these be solved within dokieli?
